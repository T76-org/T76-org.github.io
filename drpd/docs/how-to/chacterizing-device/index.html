Characterizing USB-PD source and sinks

Dr. PD can be used to characterize the behavior of a USB-PD device to understand how it behaves in different scenarios, and to identify any issues or anomalies in its behavior. This can be useful both for debugging purposes and to understand how a particular device behaves.

There are, fundamentally, two ways to characterize a device with Dr. PD:

  • For sources, you can use Sink mode and an electronic load to determine how the source behaves under different load conditions, and to identify any issues with its power delivery capabilities. For power banks, the energy and power accumulators can be used to determine the total capacity of the power bank, and to identify any issues with its energy delivery capabilities.
  • For sinks, you can use Observer mode to monitor the amount of energy required to charge a device, and to identify any issues with its power consumption. For example, you can use Dr. PD to monitor the charging of a smartphone, and to identify any issues with its charging behavior, such as excessive power consumption or overheating.
If you don't have an electronic load

An electronic load will give you the highest degree of control over load conditions; more advanced electronic loads can even simulate dynamic load conditions and can be controlled programmatically to automate testing. However, they can be expensive and may not be accessible to everyone.

If you don't have an electronic load, you can use a USB-PD sink device (such as a laptop or a smartphone) to draw power from your sources, and use Dr. PD in Observer mode to monitor the energy delivered by the power bank. This method gives you less control over the load conditions, but it can still provide useful information about the power bank's capacity and performance.

warning

Dr. PD implements its own set of safety mechanisms to protect both itself and your devices. Make sure that the overvoltage and overcurrent protection thresholds are set so that they do not interfere with your measurements.

Testing a source's power delivery and safety mechanisms​

USB-PD compliance requires that sources implement certain safety mechanisms to protect against overvoltage, overcurrent, and other abnormal conditions. Dr. PD can be used alongside an electronic load to test these safety mechanisms by simulating different scenarios and observing how the source responds:

  1. Place Dr. PD in Sink mode, and connect the source you want to test to Port 1 (the left port).
  2. Connect an electronic load to the banana jacks.
  3. Ask Dr. PD to negotiate a power contract with the source, and select a power profile that matches your requirements.
  4. Turn on capture mode to start monitoring the analog VBUS information and the USB-PD messages exchanged between the source and Dr. PD.
  5. Gradually increase the load on the electronic load, and observe how the source responds to the increasing power demand. Once the load exceeds the power contract, the source should respond by either limiting the current or (more commonly) by shutting down the power delivery to protect itself and the connected devices. You can use the analog trace to see exactly when the protection mechanism kicks in, as well as the current level at which it happens.
tip

USB-PD is pretty forgiving when it comes to overcurrent conditions, and you may find that some sources will allow the current to exceed the power contract for a short period of time before shutting down. This is because USB-PD allows for a certain amount of tolerance in the power delivery, and some sources may be designed to handle short-term overcurrent conditions without triggering their protection mechanisms. However, if you continue to increase the load, you should eventually see the source shut down the power delivery to protect itself and the connected devices.

If this behaviour is not acceptable for your application, and the source supports at least one PPS or AVS contract, these allow you to have more granular control over the voltage and current levels, and to avoid triggering the protection mechanisms of the source.

Testing a battery bank's capacity​

The batteries in a power bank degrade over time, and their capacity decreases as they are used. Dr. PD can be used to test the capacity of a power bank by measuring the amount of energy it can deliver before it is depleted:

  1. Place Dr. PD in Sink mode, and connect the fully-charged power bank you want to test to Port 1 (the left port).
  2. Connect an electronic load to the banana jacks, and set it to draw a constant current that is within the power bank's specifications.
  3. Reset the energy accumulator in Dr. PD to start measuring the energy delivered by the power bank from the moment you start the test.
  4. Turn on capture mode to start monitoring the analog VBUS information and the USB-PD messages exchanged between the power bank and Dr. PD.
  5. Start the electronic load, and let it draw power from the power bank until it is depleted. You can use the energy accumulator in Dr. PD to measure the total amount of energy delivered by the power bank before it is depleted. This will give you an estimate of the power bank's capacity, and you can compare it to the manufacturer's specifications to see how much the capacity has degraded over time.

You can repeat this process with different power contracts and current levels to see how the power bank performs under different conditions, and to identify any issues with its energy delivery capabilities. For example, you may find that the power bank delivers less energy at higher current levels, which could indicate that the batteries are degrading or that there are issues with the power delivery circuitry.

tip

Remember that a power bank's stated capacity is usually given in mAh at a specific voltage (often 3.7V, which is the nominal voltage of the internal lithium-ion batteries). This doesn't mean that the power bank will deliver that capacity at the USB output voltage (5V, 9V, etc.), due to the efficiency losses in the voltage conversion process. When testing a power bank's capacity, it's important to take into account the voltage at which you are drawing power, and to convert the measured energy back to mAh at the nominal voltage if you want to compare it to the manufacturer's specifications.
