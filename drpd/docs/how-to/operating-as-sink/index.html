Operating Dr. PD as a sink

In addition to observer mode, Dr. PD can also be used in Sink Mode, where it acts as a USB-PD sink device that can be powered by a compatible source.

This mode allows you to safely test and characterize source devices and cables without risking damage to your actual devices, and with more control over power contracts and energy use than you would have with a real sink.

Set up​

To enter sink mode, click on Mode -> Select Mode -> Sink from the menu, or press the S key on your keyboard. You should see the Mode indicator in the control app's header change to "Sink," and the Status indicator will read "Unattached" until you connect a source device.

Connect your source device to Port 1 (the left port) using a USB-C cable of your choice. If all goes well, you should see the Status indicator change to “Attached,” and the VBUS voltage and Power Profile indicators will show the current power contract between the source and Dr. PD, which will normally be the default 5V/3A contract that most sources will offer to a sink that does not have any special capabilities.

Changing the power contract​

In sink mode, Dr. PD takes note of the contracts that the source offers, and allows you to establish a new contract with the source by selecting one of the offered contracts from the Power Profile selection dialog (Mode -> Choose Power Contract…). This allows you to test the behavior of the source under different power conditions, and to perform energy measurements at different power levels.

Drawing power from the source​

In sink mode, you can use the banana connectors to monitor the VBUS voltage with an external voltmeter, and to draw current from the source using an external load—for example, to perform energy measurements or test the source's protection mechanisms.

note

In this mode, Port 2 is disconnected from the USB-PD bus and VBUS line in order to protect any device that you might have inadvertently left connected to that port, and so you will not be able to use Port 2 for any USB-PD communication or power delivery while in sink mode.

Safety mechanisms

In addition to the protection mechanisms implemented by your source device, Dr. PD implements its own set of overvoltage/overcurrent protections. You can use them in tandem with native protections in order to make sure that you don't damage your source device or Dr. PD itself while testing, but Dr. PD does not automatically set any protection mechanisms for you (beyond the default 60V / 6A limits that are hardcoded into the firmware to prevent damage to Dr. PD itself), so you should make sure to set appropriate limits for your tests in order to avoid damage to your source device or Dr. PD.

Using PPS and AVS contracts​

The USB-PD protocol includes some special types of power contracts that allow for more granular control over the voltage and current levels, which can be useful for testing sources that support these contracts:

  • PPS (Programmable Power Supply) contracts allow the sink to request specific voltage and current levels within a certain range, which can be useful for testing sources that support PPS and for avoiding triggering the protection mechanisms of the source when drawing power at higher levels.
  • AVS (Adjustable Voltage Supply) contracts similarly allow the sink to request specific voltage levels within a certain range, but they are designed for higher voltage levels (up to 48V) and are typically used for applications like USB-PD power delivery to laptops and other high-power devices.

Dr. PD is fully compatible with both PPS and AVS. If your source supports PPS or AVS contracts, you can use them to test the source's behavior under different voltage and current conditions, and to perform energy measurements at different power levels without triggering the source's protection mechanisms.

Support for EPR​

Recent versions of the USB-PD protocols have introduced Extended Power Range (EPR) contracts, which allow for power delivery at voltages up to 48V and currents up to 5A. Dr. PD is compatible with EPR, and we designed its hardware specifically to support voltage and current ranges in excess of the USB-PD 3.1 specification in order to allow you to test EPR sources and cables without risking damage to Dr. PD itself.

warning

At 48V and 5A, high-end EPR sources pack quite the punch and can deliver some 240W of power (in our experience, they will often even exceed this level by a small amount). This is more than enough to cause serious damage to devices that are not designed to handle such power levels, and even cause a fire if something goes wrong.

When you test EPR sources at high power levels, make sure to use appropriate safety precautions, such as using a high-quality USB-C cable that is rated for EPR, making sure that your source and Dr. PD are on a non-flammable surface, and keeping a fire extinguisher nearby just in case.
