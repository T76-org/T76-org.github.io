Measurement and safety guardrails

Dr. PD makes USB-PD behavior easier to see, but high-power USB-C testing is still live power work. Use known-good cables, an appropriate load, intentional protection thresholds, and external instruments when the result matters.

Electrical and measurement ranges​

Use these limits when planning a setup or interpreting a capture.

ItemValueNote
VBUS voltage measurement0-60 VStay inside the instrument limit and the ratings of the source, cable, load, and fixture.
VBUS current measurement-5 A to +5 ACurrent direction depends on the test mode and power flow. Confirm sign convention before comparing results.
Display and command resolution10 mV / 10 mAResolution is not the same as absolute accuracy.
Preliminary factory-calibrated accuracyTypical +/-1% voltage and +/-2% current, full-scaleThese specifications are preliminary, typical, and subject to change. Cross-check when accuracy matters.
Sink requestsUp to 48 V / 5 A / 240 WRequires compatible protocol state, source capability, cable capability, load, and EPR/PPS/AVS support where applicable.
OVP/OCP thresholdsUp to 60 V / 6 AThresholds are protection settings, not operating recommendations.

Reading captures carefully​

Dr. PD correlates decoded USB-PD traffic with VBUS voltage and current readings. That is useful for attach, contract changes, load steps, disconnects, hard resets, and protection events.

It helps to keep three kinds of evidence separate:

  • Protocol evidence: messages observed, decoded, filtered, exported, or timed in the Message Log.
  • Electrical readings: VBUS voltage, current, power, charge, or energy reported during the same interval.
  • External confirmation: what a DMM, oscilloscope, electronic load, or power analyzer measured at the same point in the setup.

For timing-sensitive or signal-integrity questions, use an oscilloscope or logic analyzer with the relevant CC/VBUS nodes. A decoded message log is strong protocol evidence, but it is not an analog signal-integrity measurement by itself.

Calibration caveats​

Dr. PD ships factory-calibrated, and the current preliminary typical accuracy targets are +/-1% voltage and +/-2% current full-scale. Those values are not a blanket guarantee for every setup, environmental condition, or future hardware revision.

Calibration affects all measurements and protections. If calibration data is wrong, VBUS readings can be wrong and OVP/OCP decisions can be wrong. Before changing calibration, read the calibration guide and use stable reference equipment.

For absolute accuracy work, compare more than one operating point. A single 5 V reading does not characterize the 0-60 V voltage range, and a single load point does not characterize the current range.

OVP/OCP behavior​

Dr. PD provides configurable overvoltage protection (OVP) and overcurrent protection (OCP). These protections disconnect power delivery rather than regulate it:

  • OVP/OCP are cut-off protections, not voltage clamps or current limiters.
  • Thresholds can be configured up to 60 V for OVP and 6 A for OCP.
  • A triggered OVP or OCP condition latches and requires a manual reset before normal operation resumes.
  • Threshold settings are stored on the device, so check the active limits before changing the setup.
  • The protections depend on the same measurement calibration used for displayed VBUS readings.

Choose thresholds for the specific source, cable, load, and expected contract. If a protection trip occurs, check the requested contract, cable, load behavior, Dr. PD calibration, and independent measurements before drawing conclusions.

EPR and high-power cautions​

Sink mode can request high-power USB-PD contracts up to 48 V / 5 A / 240 W when the source, cable, protocol negotiation, and load all support that condition. That is enough power to damage equipment or start a fire if the bench setup is wrong.

Before attempting EPR, AVS, PPS, or other high-power tests:

  • Use a cable rated for the requested current and mode.
  • Confirm the source actually advertises the requested contract.
  • Use an external load rated for the requested voltage, current, and power.
  • Set OVP/OCP thresholds intentionally rather than relying on defaults.
  • Keep the test attended and stop if cables, connectors, the source, the load, or Dr. PD heat unexpectedly.

If a high-power request fails, the limiting factor may be the cable, contract selection, EPR entry state, load, protection settings, or thermal behavior, not just the source.

Port 2 lockout and reverse polarity​

When Dr. PD operates as a sink on Port 1, Port 2 is disconnected from the USB-PD bus and VBUS line. This lockout protects a device that might have been left on Port 2, but a simple setup with unused devices removed is easier to understand.

The banana connectors include reverse-polarity protection for cases where an external source or load is connected backwards. If it trips, Dr. PD disconnects the banana connector VBUS path; the control app does not present this as an OVP/OCP event. Treat an unexpected 0 V reading at the banana connectors as a reason to stop and inspect wiring before continuing.

External cross-checks​

Useful cross-checks include:

  • Comparing Dr. PD voltage readings with a DMM at the banana connectors.
  • Comparing Dr. PD current readings with the electronic load readback or a current probe/shunt suitable for the expected current.
  • Capturing VBUS and CC with an oscilloscope while Dr. PD records decoded protocol traffic.
  • Recording cable identity, source model, selected contract, load setting, protection thresholds, firmware/software version, and calibration state.

Dr. PD can make USB-PD behavior visible and repeatable. The bench setup, external validation, and safety decisions still matter.
